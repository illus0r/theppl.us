An unrepresentative Congress

Posted by Corey Conner on Jul 6, 2012 in Blog | 0 comments

Photo Courtesy of Andrew Malone

Despite the United States’ history as a representative democracy, the current congress isn’t much like its people.

Cord Jefferson, with Good, recently looked at what congress would look like if it really represented America.

Almost 100 years after the first woman was elected to congress, there are only 91 women serving compared to 448 men.

Racial diversity is also lacking. Caucasians are over-represented, while every other race is under-represented.

As Jefferson pointed out, even economically, Congress doesn’t represent the norm.

Our senators and representatives are also wholly different from most Americans in terms of wealth. We’ve said this before, but it bears repeating: The average American’s net worth is $96,000. But the average Senator’s net worth? $13.4 million. For House members that sum drops to “just” $5 million.

57 members of Congress are part of the 1%, but will a candidate’s personal finances trump the all important issues of jobs and the economy at large?

Not in the opinion of Washington Post columnist Aaron Blake.

So should we expect Americans to start voting out the wealthy and voting in the middle class? Not exactly.

The realities of campaigning make it a rich person’s enterprise. Candidates often have to have enough wealth so that they can leave their jobs to campaign full-time, and it always helps to be able to put millions of your own money into a campaign.

But the fact that people are learning that their Congress is getting richer, even in tough economic times, bodes ill for whatever hope legislators had for returning to the good graces of the American public.

Despite this, occupy-inspired candidates, such as George Martinez in New York’s 7th congressional district, are struggling to move past protest movements and into electoral politics.

So, while Congress’s personal wealth may not inspire much voter enthusiasm, it could help sustain the values of a struggling Occupy movement. As the rich become richer and the rest, poorer, the discrepancies between Congress and the people may become exasperated as they move to tackle issues such as $1 trillion in student loan debt heading into election season.

Historically, Congress hasn’t been an edifice of diversity, and there is no indication that drastic change is around the corner. However, a prolonged state of depression might cause voters to look for a more accurate representation of themselves.

An Exciting Change Brings CarolinaFest 2012 to Uptown Charlotte

Posted by Justin Ruckman on Jun 26, 2012 in Blog | 0 comments

Photo Property of CLT BLog

The Charlotte in 2012 convention host committee opted to move the location of CarolinaFest 2012 to Tryon Street in Uptown Charlotte. The committee had originally decided to hold the event in the Charlotte Motor Speedway this year but they believe this new change better meets the needs of the community.

Dan Murray, the executive director of the charlotte in 2012 host committee, listed a few of the benefits this new change will bring, during a press conference earlier this afternoon. Some of those benefits include more convenient transportation options as well as access to the caucus meetings that will be taking place in nearby locations. Dan Murray also believes hosting CarolinaFest 2012 in Uptown Charlotte will help local businesses that will be open to the public during the events.

For more information, visit charlottein2012.com

Surrounding Charlotte cities prepare to help handle DNC protesters

Posted by Brian Zarbock on Jun 15, 2012 in Blog | 2 comments

Crowd control isn’t something new to Charlotte area police, with their most recent experience being  the Occupy Charlotte protest group. The coming September Democratic National Convention however might prove too much to handle for Charlotte police especially given the aftermath of the NATO riots in Chicago.

Chicago Police during NATO riots in Chicago- Courtesy vonderauvisuals

 

Lake Norman News, a Charlotte Observer affiliate is reporting that cities surrounding Mecklenburg are training officers for any unrest during the DNC. These cities include,  Huntersville, Cornelius, Davidson and Mooresville. None of these cities have any real experience with crowd control in the past with the exception of Huntersville who dealt with a “religious group” protesting abortions at a local highschool. Despite the quiet history of these cities Huntersville Police Chief Philip Potter is considering bringing in a specialist to train officers how to handle protesters.

Not all of the officers will be protecting the home front during the DNC. Davidson has two officers coming in to work the bike patrol during the DNC with the expectation that more will be added in the future. In the past protesters might plan on meeting in one place but using the instantaneous communication of social media they could change the locations in a heartbeat so nearby cities will have to be alert the entire time.  There is a possibility these cities will see no action at all but the DNC will still bring an influx to surrounding areas which could spell some nasty traffic tickets for any wary out of towner.
